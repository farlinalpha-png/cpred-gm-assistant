a bit at a time.

Stealth Operations

Not every part of every mission involves loud guns 
and explosions. Sometimes, a crew needs to go quiet. 
Maybe they’re breaking into a Neocorp’s HQ and 
don’t want to bring a hundred Corporate cops down 
on their heads. Maybe they’re running a con or pulling 
a heist. Whatever the case, autofire, massive linear 
frames, and grenades aren’t always the answer. That’s 
what Going Quiet is all about.
We’ve broken this article up into three sections. The 
first is Flash of Luck, an optional ruleset first published 
in Tales of the RED: Hope Reborn. It helps prevent 
“planning paralysis” by allowing Players to spend Luck 
and retcon a past event into existence. Next, Stealth 
Netrunning. You read that right. We’re finally providing Netrunners the rules they need to slip through a NET 
Architecture undetected. Finally, Harry the Shrimp’s 
Gear Guide is a selection of equipment curated especially for stealth ops by a legendary grifter.
Stealth runs might be hard … but they’re damn fun. 
Good luck, choomba!
Flash of Luck
flas hofluck
In the real world, leaders spend an astonishing amount 
of time planning missions, attempting to account for 
every contingency. In a TTRPG, that can get boring 
quickly, leading to a whole session (or more!) spent 
planning for action instead of taking action.
Flash of Luck gives Players a safety net, allowing them 
to spend Luck Points (see CP:R page 130) to retcon a 
past event into existence, providing an advantage when 
the unforeseen occurs. This new rule helps prevent heists, 
infiltration, and con jobs from transforming into hours-long 
planning sessions or absolute disasters due to not anticipating one specific element of the gig. It might be too 
powerful to use during more combat-oriented missions, so 
GMs, feel free to only allow the use of this rule when you 
feel it adds to the game and doesn’t unbalance it.

The Rules
The rules are as follows. Refer to the Flash of Luck table 
for specific guidelines on building a flashback.
• A Player can only use Flash of Luck once per Beat 
and no more than three times per Mission.
• Performing a Flash of Luck is a Standard Action if 
done during combat.
• The Player must spend Luck Points as indicated 
on the Flash of Luck table and then describe 
the action they took in the past, tell when 
it occurred, and explain how it helps in the 
current situation. Players still need to spend the 
appropriate amount of money and succeed at 
any Checks needed to make the action viable. 
If they cannot buy the item in question (due to 
GM fiat or the economy rules), they get their 
Luck Points back. If they fail Check(s), the Flash 
of Luck still occurs, showing they tried the 
action but failed — they cannot try again. This 
counts against the number of times the rule can 
be used per Beat/Mission, and the spent Luck 
is not refunded.
• GMs always have the right to veto a Flash of Luck 
if they feel it is too powerful or impossible. For 
example, you can’t slip sugar into the gas tank of a 
car you couldn’t have known would be in the race, 
and no amount of prep will let you hide a standard 
sniper rifle in your Rockerboy’s skintight bodysuit. 
Example: A security team has cornered Racer 
(played by Kennedy) and Torch (played by Isaac) 
in a Corporate facility. Kennedy says, “We can both 
see in the dark. I wish there were a way to turn off 
the lights so we can get an advantage.” Isaac slides 
2 Luck Points over to the GM and states, “Outside in 
the alley, before we slipped into the building, Torch 
jimmied open a control panel and hardwired a remote 
control device into the wiring. When I tell Beatrice, my 
Agent’s pseudo-AI, the codeword, she’ll activate it, and 
the lights will shut off.”

Stealth Netrunning
s t e al t hn e t running
Successfully pulling off a Netrun without being detected is 
all about maintaining stealth across multiple obstacles in a 
NET Architecture. Mechanically, you’ll need to successfully 
Quietly Jack In to establish stealth, and Evade Detection 
from any Black ICE, Demons, and Netrunners you encounter in the Architecture for the duration of your run.
Jacking In … Quietly
To establish stealth in a NET Architecture, you have 
to spend an additional NET Action when you Jack In. 
When you do, you roll a contested Interface Check 
(Interface + 1d10 vs Interface + 1d10) against all 
Watchers (Demons, enemy Netrunners … and 
other entities with an Interface Rank) currently in the 
Architecture. If you beat them all, you successfully 
establish stealth and enter the system undetected.
Example: Redeye wants to stealth her way through a 
small Corporate facility’s NET Architecture. There are 
two Watchers currently in the system: an Imp Demon and 
a Corporate Netrunner. Redeye’s Player rolls Interface 
(4) + 1d10 (8) for a total of 12. The GM rolls for the 
Imp (Interface [3] + 1d10 [7] = 10) and the Corporate 
Netrunner (Interface [4] + 1d10 [2] = 6). Since Redeye 
beat them both, she goes undetected. Neither the Imp 
nor the Corporate Netrunner knows she Jacked In.
Benefits of Stealth
Being stealthed in a NET Architecture allows a Netrunner 
to operate as they would normally, with the benefit of 
being able to avoid combat and preventing Watchers 
from sounding an alarm. However, there are two major 
limits: taking possession of a Control Node always 
breaks stealth, as does directly interacting with (for 
example, attacking) Black ICE or a Watcher. Leaving 
Viruses and stealing lucrative Files are fair game, though 
if you leave a flashy Virus, you’ll obviously draw attention as soon as it activates. If you’re smart, by that time, 
you’ll be out the door … or better yet, long gone.
Breaking Stealth
When stealth is broken, all Watchers in a NET 
Architecture become aware of the Netrunner’s presence, 
and all Black ICE react to them as they normally would.
Black ICE and Stealth
When a Netrunner encounters a Black ICE while in 
stealth, they roll Interface + any bonus to Cloak Checks 
+ 1d10 vs the Black ICE’s Perception + 1d10 instead of 
performing a Speed Check.
If the Netrunner is successful, the Black ICE does not 
spot them and doesn’t enter the Initiative Queue at all. 
Stealth is maintained. If the Netrunner fails, stealth is 
broken, they suffer the effect of the Black ICE as if they 
failed a Speed Check, and the Black ICE jumps to the 
top of the Initiative Queue. A Black ICE unaware of a 
Netrunner due to success at stealth does not count as 
“encountered” if that Netrunner is unsafely Jacked Out.
Example: Redeye encounters an Asp on the third floor 
of a NET Architecture. The GM calls for an opposed 
Check. Redeye’s Player rolls her Interface (4) + the 
Cloak bonus from an active Eraser Program (2) + 1d10 
(3) for a total of 9. The GM rolls the Asp’s Perception 
(4) + 1d10 and gets an 8. The GM describes the Asp 
peeking up its head for an instant, tongue slithering in 
and out of its digital mouth as it looks slowly around 
the floor, before sinking back to rest. Redeye stealths 
by to the next floor.
Watchers and Stealth
When a Netrunner encounters a Watcher, a quiet 
battle of Netrunning ability takes place. The Netrunner 
rolls Interface + any bonus to Cloak Checks + 1d10 
vs the Watcher’s Interface + any bonus to Pathfinder 
Checks + 1d10. This does not take a NET Action for 
any participant. If the Netrunner is successful, stealth 
is maintained. If the Netrunner fails, stealth is broken.
On their Turn, a Watcher in the same NET 
Architecture as a stealthed Netrunner can use a NET 
Action to make a Pathfinder Check and search for 
hidden enemies. They can only do this once per Turn. 
To do this, they roll Interface + any bonus to Pathfinder 
Checks + 1d10 vs the stealthed Netrunner’s Interface 
+ any bonus to Cloak Checks + 1d10. If the Watcher is 
successful, stealth is broken.

Reestablishing Stealth
Once stealth is broken, the Netrunner must Jack Out and 
Quietly Jack In again to attempt to re-establish it. You 
can’t unsound an alarm, though, so the damage may 
already be done. If you’re trying to hide from an enemy 
Netrunner after they caught you just a few seconds 
earlier, the GM is perfectly within their right to assign 
your Netrunning stealth Checks a penalty (see CP:R 
page 130).

▶ Binary Compound
Cost: 50eb (Costly) per tube
A classic thief’s tool, upgraded by tech for the modern 
age. The tube comes with two nozzles. Each nozzle 
expels a chemical that, while harmless on its own, 
combines with its mate to create a nasty compound 
capable of eating through anything.
It takes 6 seconds (2 Rounds) to apply the Binary 
Compound to a 2 m/yd by 2 m/yd section of cover. Once 
it is applied, the Compound begins damaging the targeted 
section of cover at a rate of 6d6 damage per Round. If the 
cover is layered, the Compound continues to eat through 
the next layer of cover once it is done with the previous 
layer. Binary Compound becomes inert and no longer 
does damage 18 seconds (6 Rounds) after it is applied.
▶ Branded Jumpsuit
Cost: Variable
Forget Ghostsuits. The easiest way to keep invisible in 
most surroundings is to look like you belong. Get the 
right color jumpsuit fitted with the proper patches, and 
most people won’t even give you a second peep.
A jumpsuit or similar uniform identical to those worn by 
a specific organization’s construction, janitorial, food 
service, or other crew members. The cost depends on 
the security of the organization.
• Low Security (convenience store): 50eb (Premium)
• Moderate Security (construction site or hospital): 
100eb (Expensive)
• High Security (smaller Corporation): 500eb (Very 
Expensive)
• Extreme Security (Megacorporation): 1,000eb 
(Luxury)
▶ Concealment Sleeve
Cost: 500eb (Very Expensive)
You’ve got to sneak into a Corporate joint and extract 
a target. For that, you’ll need weapons and tools. You 
might be thinking, if the gear’s inside my chrome, security will miss it, right? Wrong! Plenty of Corps shell out 
for cyberware detectors at the front door. There’s a 
solution, though. Find a Tech with the right skills, and 
they’ll fit a Concealment Sleeve over your cyberlimb. 
Then you can walk through with no problem.
A special sleeve, fitted over a cybernetic limb, containing ultra-thin emitters wrapped in sculpted RealSkinnTM. 
When wearing a Concealment Sleeve, a user’s cyberlimb reads as biological to a cyberware scanner. Each 
sleeve must be custom-fitted to a specific cyberlimb. 
Once fitted, a sleeve cannot be adjusted to work for 
any other limb.

▶ Handprint Glove
Cost: 500eb (Very Expensive)
You infiltrate enough secure joints, you’ll run into a 
handprint scanner eventually. The technology’s old and 
unreliable compared to modern security measures, but 
also cheap … and Execs sure do love cheap!
A glove made from RealskinnTM imprinted with a specific handprint (including fingerprints). While the glove 
will fool a security scanner, it will not leave actual prints 
behind on surfaces. Each Handprint Glove must be 
custom ordered, and the print being duplicated must 
be provided to the Tech making it.
▶ Handprint Scanner
Cost: 20eb (Everyday)
To make a Handprint Glove, you need a record of 
someone’s handprint. With your Agent, it’s duck soup. 
That means easy, kid. You can scan the hand itself, 
or blow some fine particulate matter onto prints left 
behind on a smooth surface, and scan that instead.
App. To record someone’s handprint directly, the user 
must be in Melee Range and do nothing but scan a 
target’s exposed hand (palm side) with the App for 9 
seconds (3 Rounds). This time need not be consecutive. 
Scanning a target for a Round is an Action and requires 
the user to succeed at a DV13 Brawling Check. If the 
target is unconscious, bound, or otherwise unable to 
move, the user succeeds at this Check automatically. 
The user can also scan prints left behind on smooth 
surfaces. If the print in question is complete (the whole 
hand pressed down on a surface), this only requires 
one scan. Otherwise, the user must compile the target’s 
handprint from at least five different prints. Each time 
the user scans a print, they must succeed at a DV13 
Criminology Check to capture it for use.
▶ Laser Microphone
Cost: 100eb (Premium)
A surprising number of buildings in Night City still have 
glass windows. Don’t know why. Those things are a 
security hazard and outside ain’t that glam, anyway.
Laser Mics generate a beam of amplified light (aka 
a laser) that can be aimed at a pane of glass up to 
300 m/yds away. Once the beam is set, the device 
reads the vibrations in the window caused by sound 
in the room beyond, then translates them into audible 
noise and conversation for its user. The Microphone’s 
laser is normally invisible but can be seen if it travels 
through mist, smoke, fog, or similar substances. Laser 
Mics can read vibrations from Thin Bulletproof Glass 
but not Thick Bulletproof Glass.
▶ Militech M97 Ghostsuit
Cost: 5,000eb (Luxury)
This sort of thing ain’t for me, but I know snipers and a few 
thieves who swear by it. Ghostsuits are full-body coveralls 
incorporating liquid crystal displays, fiber-optic digitization systems, and mini-camera technology. It scans your 
surroundings, then changes to match it and make you as 
close to invisible as modern science can manage.
When the user remains still for 1 minute (20 Rounds), 
they can activate a cloak that hides them from all 
visual senses, including Low Light/Infrared/UV, unless 
the perceiver succeeds at a DV17 Perception Check. 
Activating the Cloak does not require an Action. The 
cloak is deactivated automatically if the user moves 
further than 2 m/yds (1 square) within 3 seconds (1 
Round).
▶ Nymph Perfume
Cost: 500eb (Expensive) per bottle
Back in the 2020s, Biotechnica claimed their Nymph 
Perfume line would “stimulate the sexual centers of the 
brain” via special pheromones. Turns out that was a 
bunch of baloney. The perfume’s just a mild, aerosolized euphoric. Still useful in a con, though. Just make 
sure it don’t work on you, capisce?
A spritz of Nymph Perfume lingers on a surface for 
1 hour. Anyone who spends more than 9 seconds (3 
Rounds) within 6 m/yds of the perfume’s source must 
succeed at a DV13 Resist Torture/Drugs Check or 
become slightly confused and take a -2 penalty to 
Human Perception Checks for 1 hour. A person who 
wears the perfume is considered to be within 6 m/
yds of themselves for this purpose. Sufficient protection, such as Nasal Filters or an Anti-Smog Breathing 
Mask, prevents inhaling the perfume. Nymph Perfume 
is considered a drug for the purposes of administering Rapidetox (see CP:R page 150). A full bottle of 
Nymph Perfume contains 50 spritzes.

Overlays a fake retina pattern on the user’s eye, allowing them to fool some forms of security. Each Retina

▶ Retina Scanner
Cost: 50eb (Costly)
Of course, to make a Retina Lens, you need a retinal 
pattern first. Back in the day, you needed an expensive, 
specialized device to scan someone’s eye. Now, you 
just need an app and an Agent.
App. To record someone’s retinal pattern, the user must 
be in Melee Range and do nothing but scan a target’s 
exposed eye with the App for 15 seconds (5 Rounds). 
This time need not be consecutive. Scanning a target for 
a Round is an Action and requires the user to succeed 
at a DV13 Brawling Check. If the target is unconscious, 
bound, or otherwise unable to move, the user succeeds 
at this Check automatically. Users with Internal Agents 
can record retinal patterns, so long as they also have 
MicroVideo installed in a Cybereye or Smart Glasses.
▶ Translation Chip
Cost: 1,000eb (Very Expensive)
Install: N/A
Humanity Loss: 7 (2d6)
Raven Microcyb just released these swanky things onto 
the market. Real-time translation devices. As you see 
or hear words, the chip translates into the language 
of your choice and projects that text onto your chyron. 
Chipware. Requires Chyron. Any words in a language heard or read by the user are translated into a

▶ Zetatech Tank Commander Headset
Cost: 100eb (Premium)
When you’re trying for a clean sneak, sound’s your

noise, so they won’t hear your mate’s voice, either. 
Zetatech makes the best one, in my humble.
A wrap-around throat microphone and attached 
earbud that can be linked wirelessly to any Agent or 
radio. Detecting sound going into or coming out of a 
Stealth Headset is impossible without some form of 
Amplified Hearing.
